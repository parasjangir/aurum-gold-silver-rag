## What hallmarking is

Hallmarking is a third-party certification of the purity (fineness) of a
precious-metal article. In India it is administered by the Bureau of Indian
Standards (BIS). A hallmark assures the buyer that the gold content of the
article meets the purity stamped on it.

## Mandatory hallmarking

Hallmarking of gold jewellery and artefacts became mandatory in a phased
manner starting June 2021, and the covered districts and item categories have
been expanded in subsequent phases. Jewellers selling hallmarked gold must be
registered with BIS. Mandatory hallmarking means a registered jeweller may not
sell covered gold jewellery without a valid hallmark.

## The three marks on a current hallmark

Since the introduction of the HUID system, a BIS hallmark on gold carries
three components:

1. **BIS logo** — a triangular mark identifying the Bureau of Indian Standards.
2. **Purity / fineness grade** — the karatage and corresponding fineness, for
   example `22K916` for 22-karat gold (916 parts per thousand).
3. **HUID (Hallmark Unique Identification)** — a six-digit alphanumeric code.
   Every hallmarked article gets its own unique HUID, recorded in the BIS
   database, which makes each piece individually traceable.

Older hallmarks (pre-HUID) also showed the assaying-and-hallmarking centre's
mark and the jeweller's identification mark.

## Assaying and Hallmarking Centres

Hallmarking is physically carried out at BIS-recognised Assaying and
Hallmarking Centres (AHCs). The centre tests the metal (assaying), and on a
passing result the article is laser-marked with the hallmark and assigned its
HUID.

## Why it matters to a buyer

A hallmark protects the buyer from under-karatage — being sold, say, 18K gold
priced as 22K. Because the HUID is unique and database-backed, a buyer can
verify a specific article rather than trusting only the showroom's word.
